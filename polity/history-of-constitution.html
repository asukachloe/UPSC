SCOPE, TIME FRAME, HOW TO READ THIS SHEET

  • This sheet covers the long constitutional history of India from the coming of European trading powers to the commencement of the Constitution of India on 26 January 1950.
  • It is not limited only to the Constituent Assembly period.
  • It includes the gradual constitutional, administrative, legislative, and political evolution under colonial rule.
  • In UPSC terms, this means:
    • coming of Europeans
    • East India Company rule
    • Crown rule
    • constitutional reforms
    • growth of representative institutions
    • nationalist constitutional demands
    • Cabinet Mission
    • Constituent Assembly
    • Independence
    • Constitution-making
    • enforcement of the Constitution in 1950
  • “History of Indian Constitution” in exam terms usually begins not from 1946, but from British constitutional developments in India.
  • The most important pattern to remember is this:
    • trade entry
    • Company expansion
    • British parliamentary control
    • Crown takeover
    • representative reforms
    • nationalist pressure
    • transfer of power
    • Constitution framing
  • UPSC trap: constitutional history is not just from 1946 onward; it begins with British legal and administrative intervention in India.

EUROPEANS IN INDIA, INITIAL ENTRY, CONSTITUTIONAL RELEVANCE

  • Portuguese were the first Europeans to arrive in India by sea route in the modern age.
  • Vasco da Gama reached Calicut in 1498.
  • This is one of the highest-yield opening facts.
  • Portuguese established early coastal power.
  • Goa became an important Portuguese center.
  • Dutch also came to India.
  • Danish also came.
  • French also came.
  • English East India Company came as a trading body.
  • Among these, the English gradually became politically dominant.
  • Constitutional history of modern India is mainly tied to British power, especially the English East India Company and later the British Crown.
  • UPSC trap: Portuguese came first, but British constitutional history in India is tied mainly to English East India Company and British Parliament.

EAST INDIA COMPANY, FORMATION, EARLY CHARTERS

  • English East India Company was formed on 31 December 1600.
  • Queen Elizabeth I granted the Charter of 1600.
  • The Charter gave the Company monopoly of trade with the East.
  • This charter is constitutionally important because British authority in India began through royal charter.
  • Company was initially a commercial corporation, not a sovereign ruler.
  • It gradually acquired political and territorial power.
  • The Company established factories in India.
  • Surat was an early English factory.
  • Madras, Bombay, and Calcutta later became major centers.
  • These three became Presidencies.
  • Madras Presidency was one major administrative unit.
  • Bombay Presidency was another.
  • Bengal Presidency became the most important.
  • Company rule evolved from trade to territorial administration after political and military victories.
  • Battle of Plassey in 1757 was a turning point.
  • Robert Clive defeated Siraj-ud-Daulah at Plassey.
  • Battle of Buxar in 1764 was another decisive turning point.
  • At Buxar, the Company defeated Mir Qasim, Shuja-ud-Daula, and Shah Alam II.
  • After Buxar, Company authority became much stronger.
  • In 1765, the Company obtained Diwani rights of Bengal, Bihar, and Orissa from Mughal emperor Shah Alam II.
  • Diwani rights meant right to collect revenue.
  • This transformed the Company from a trader into a territorial power.
  • Constitutional history becomes sharply important after the Company starts exercising civil administration and revenue authority.
  • UPSC trap: Plassey began political dominance; Buxar consolidated it.

DUAL GOVERNMENT IN BENGAL

  • Robert Clive introduced the Dual Government in Bengal in 1765.
  • Under Dual Government, the Company got Diwani, that is revenue rights.
  • The Nawab retained Nizamat, that is nominal administrative and judicial responsibility.
  • Real power was with the Company, but responsibility was blurred.
  • This system allowed the Company to control revenue without direct accountability.
  • Dual Government led to administrative confusion and exploitation.
  • It was abolished by Warren Hastings in 1772.
  • UPSC trap: Dual Government = Clive, Bengal, 1765.
  • UPSC trap: Warren Hastings ended Dual Government.

REGULATING ACT, 1773

  • Regulating Act of 1773 was the first major step by the British Parliament to regulate the affairs of the East India Company.
  • This is one of the most important acts in constitutional history.
  • It was passed because of Company mismanagement and corruption.
  • It recognized that the Company’s political role in India required parliamentary oversight.
  • The Governor of Bengal became the Governor-General of Bengal.
  • Warren Hastings became the first Governor-General of Bengal.
  • This is a very high-yield fact.
  • The Governors of Bombay and Madras were subordinated in certain matters to the Governor-General of Bengal.
  • This was the beginning of centralization in British Indian administration.
  • An Executive Council of four members was created to assist the Governor-General.
  • Supreme Court was established at Calcutta in 1774.
  • Sir Elijah Impey became the first Chief Justice of the Supreme Court at Calcutta.
  • Regulating Act was the first time the British Parliament directly intervened in Company administration in India.
  • It did not end Company rule.
  • It began parliamentary control over Company rule.
  • UPSC trap: Regulating Act 1773 = first step of parliamentary control + first Governor-General of Bengal + Supreme Court at Calcutta.

PITTS INDIA ACT, 1784

  • Pitt’s India Act was passed in 1784.
  • It was named after William Pitt the Younger.
  • Pitt’s India Act strengthened British government control over the Company.
  • It created a system of double control.
  • The Company retained commercial functions.
  • The British government got stronger political supervision.
  • A Board of Control was created.
  • The Board of Control represented the British Crown/government.
  • The Court of Directors continued to handle Company business matters.
  • Thus Company territory in India was brought under dual system of control:
    • Court of Directors
    • Board of Control
  • Pitt’s India Act distinguished clearly between commercial and political functions.
  • It reduced Company autonomy in political matters.
  • It is one of the major landmarks in constitutional evolution.
  • UPSC trap: Pitt’s India Act 1784 created Board of Control.
  • UPSC trap: Regulating Act started parliamentary control; Pitt’s India Act made it stronger and more structured.

ACT OF 1786

  • Act of 1786 is associated with strengthening the powers of Governor-General.
  • It allowed the Governor-General to override his council in special cases.
  • This provision was connected with Lord Cornwallis.
  • This act is not as frequently asked as Regulating Act or Pitt’s India Act, but it is important in the chain of constitutional centralization.
  • UPSC trap: Act of 1786 enhanced Governor-General’s authority.

CHARTER ACTS, GENERAL IMPORTANCE

  • Charter Acts were periodic renewals of the East India Company’s charter by British Parliament.
  • These acts gradually transformed the Company from a trading corporation into an administrative agency of British rule.
  • The most important Charter Acts are:
    • Charter Act 1793
    • Charter Act 1813
    • Charter Act 1833
    • Charter Act 1853
  • UPSC trap: Charter Acts are major stages in the constitutional evolution of Company rule.

CHARTER ACT OF 1793

  • Charter Act of 1793 renewed the Company’s charter for 20 years.
  • It continued Company trade monopoly.
  • It continued the powers of the Governor-General and Council.
  • It did not bring revolutionary change like some later Charter Acts.
  • It is important mainly as a continuation stage.
  • UPSC trap: Charter Act 1793 continued Company monopoly.

CHARTER ACT OF 1813

  • Charter Act of 1813 is very important.
  • It ended the Company’s trade monopoly in India except trade in tea and trade with China.
  • This is the most important microfact.
  • It allowed Christian missionaries to come to India officially.
  • It allowed the spread of Western education influences more openly.
  • It provided for an annual sum of one lakh rupees for education.
  • This act marks a shift from pure commercial monopoly toward broader imperial governance.
  • UPSC trap: Charter Act 1813 ended Company monopoly except tea and China trade.
  • UPSC trap: one lakh rupees for education is linked with Charter Act 1813.

CHARTER ACT OF 1833

  • Charter Act of 1833 is one of the most important constitutional acts.
  • It ended the Company’s commercial activities entirely.
  • The Company became purely an administrative body.
  • This is a very high-yield fact.
  • The Governor-General of Bengal became the Governor-General of India.
  • Lord William Bentinck became the first Governor-General of India.
  • This is a very high-yield fact.
  • It centralized legislative power.
  • The Governor-General-in-Council got exclusive legislative powers for all British India.
  • The legislative powers of Bombay and Madras Presidencies were taken away.
  • This act marks a major step toward administrative and legislative centralization.
  • It attempted to introduce open competition in civil services in principle, though not fully implemented yet in practical terms.
  • It also stated that no Indian subject should be debarred from office on grounds of religion, birth, descent, etc., though actual practice remained discriminatory.
  • A Law Commission was provided for.
  • The first Law Commission under Macaulay was formed after this.
  • UPSC trap: Charter Act 1833 = first Governor-General of India + Company became purely administrative + legislative centralization.
  • UPSC trap: Lord William Bentinck = first Governor-General of India.

CHARTER ACT OF 1853

  • Charter Act of 1853 was the last Charter Act.
  • It renewed the Company’s charter without specifying a fixed period.
  • This reflected uncertainty and ongoing transition.
  • It separated the legislative and executive functions of the Governor-General’s Council for the first time in a clearer way.
  • Additional members were added for legislative purposes.
  • This is important because it was a step toward a legislative body.
  • Open competition for the Civil Services was introduced in principle.
  • This laid the foundation for the Indian Civil Service through competitive examination.
  • It marks a move toward a more bureaucratic and legislative state.
  • UPSC trap: Charter Act 1853 = last Charter Act + separation of legislative and executive functions in the council + competitive civil service principle.

GOVERNMENT OF INDIA ACT, 1858

  • Revolt of 1857 was the immediate background to the Government of India Act 1858.
  • Revolt of 1857 exposed weaknesses of Company rule.
  • Government of India Act 1858 ended East India Company rule in India.
  • This is one of the most important turning points in constitutional history.
  • India came under direct rule of the British Crown.
  • This is called the beginning of Crown rule.
  • The office of Secretary of State for India was created.
  • The Secretary of State was a British cabinet minister.
  • He was assisted by a Council of India.
  • The Governor-General of India now also became the Viceroy.
  • Viceroy meant personal representative of the British Crown.
  • Lord Canning became the first Viceroy of India.
  • This is a very high-yield fact.
  • Company’s Court of Directors and Board of Control were abolished.
  • This act transferred control from Company to Crown.
  • UPSC trap: Government of India Act 1858 ended Company rule and started Crown rule.
  • UPSC trap: Lord Canning = first Viceroy of India.
  • UPSC trap: Secretary of State for India was created by Act of 1858.

INDIAN COUNCILS ACT, 1861

  • Indian Councils Act 1861 is very important.
  • It introduced the portfolio system in administration.
  • Lord Canning is associated with introduction of portfolio system.
  • This is a high-yield fact.
  • It restored legislative powers to Bombay and Madras Presidencies to some extent.
  • Thus it partly decentralized legislation after the extreme centralization of 1833.
  • It enlarged the Viceroy’s Executive Council for legislative purposes.
  • It introduced the principle of legislative councils with non-official members.
  • Indians were nominated to legislative councils for the first time.
  • This is one of the most important microfacts.
  • However, these were nominated members, not elected.
  • The Act initiated the process of associating Indians with law-making, though in a very limited way.
  • UPSC trap: Indian Councils Act 1861 = beginning of representative association through nomination.
  • UPSC trap: portfolio system is linked with Indian Councils Act 1861 and Lord Canning.

INDIAN COUNCILS ACT, 1892

  • Indian Councils Act 1892 enlarged legislative councils further.
  • It increased the number of additional members in central and provincial councils.
  • It introduced an indirect and limited principle of election, though the word “election” was not officially used.
  • This is a major trap point.
  • Some members were recommended by bodies such as municipalities, universities, district boards, chambers of commerce, etc.
  • Legislative councils got the right to discuss the budget.
  • But they could not vote on the budget.
  • They could ask questions, subject to conditions.
  • This was still a very limited reform.
  • UPSC trap: Indian Councils Act 1892 introduced indirect election in practice, though not formally by name.
  • UPSC trap: members could discuss budget but not vote on it.

INDIAN COUNCILS ACT, 1909 / MORLEY-MINTO REFORMS

  • Indian Councils Act 1909 is also called Morley-Minto Reforms.
  • Lord Morley was Secretary of State.
  • Lord Minto was Viceroy.
  • This act significantly enlarged legislative councils.
  • It allowed for discussion of public matters more broadly.
  • Indians were included in the Viceroy’s Executive Council.
  • Satyendra Prasad Sinha became the first Indian member of the Viceroy’s Executive Council.
  • This is a high-yield fact.
  • The most controversial and famous provision was the introduction of separate electorates for Muslims.
  • This is one of the most important constitutional microfacts.
  • Separate electorates meant Muslim voters elected Muslim representatives.
  • This institutionalized communal representation.
  • The act thus gave legal form to communal politics in representation.
  • This is a key UPSC trap.
  • Provincial legislative councils were expanded.
  • Central legislature was also enlarged.
  • However, responsible government was still absent.
  • UPSC trap: Morley-Minto Reforms 1909 introduced separate electorates.
  • UPSC trap: S. P. Sinha was the first Indian in Viceroy’s Executive Council.

GROWTH OF NATIONALISM, EARLY POLITICAL DEMANDS, CONSTITUTIONAL CONTEXT

  • Indian National Congress was founded in 1885.
  • A. O. Hume was one of the key figures in its founding.
  • W. C. Bonnerjee was the first President of the INC.
  • Early Congress leaders are often called Moderates.
  • Moderates demanded constitutional reforms through petitions and dialogue.
  • Their demands included:
    • expansion of legislative councils
    • greater Indian participation in administration
    • civil service reforms
    • representative government
  • Later Extremists demanded swaraj more forcefully.
  • Constitutional reform became a major nationalist issue.
  • Partition of Bengal in 1905 intensified political mobilization.
  • Swadeshi movement followed.
  • Muslim League was founded in 1906.
  • Communal representation entered constitutional politics increasingly after 1909.
  • UPSC trap: nationalist politics and constitutional reform are deeply linked from the late 19th century onward.

GOVERNMENT OF INDIA ACT, 1919 / MONTAGU-CHELMSFORD REFORMS

  • Government of India Act 1919 is also called Montagu-Chelmsford Reforms.
  • Edwin Montagu was Secretary of State.
  • Lord Chelmsford was Viceroy.
  • The background included World War I and the August Declaration of 1917.
  • The August Declaration of 1917 promised gradual development of self-governing institutions and responsible government in India.
  • Government of India Act 1919 introduced dyarchy in the provinces.
  • Dyarchy is one of the most important microfacts in constitutional history.
  • Under dyarchy, provincial subjects were divided into:
    • transferred subjects
    • reserved subjects
  • Transferred subjects were administered by Indian ministers responsible to legislative councils.
  • Reserved subjects were administered by the Governor and executive council without responsibility to legislature.
  • Examples of transferred subjects:
    • education
    • public health
    • local self-government
    • agriculture in broad terms
  • Examples of reserved subjects:
    • law and order
    • police
    • finance
    • irrigation in many lists
  • Dyarchy applied only in provinces, not at the centre.
  • Central legislature became bicameral for the first time.
  • This is a very high-yield fact.
  • The two houses were:
    • Council of State
    • Legislative Assembly
  • Franchise was still very limited.
  • Separate electorates were extended.
  • Communal representation expanded.
  • Provincial legislative councils were enlarged.
  • Though reforms appeared significant, real responsible government was still not granted.
  • UPSC trap: Government of India Act 1919 introduced dyarchy in provinces.
  • UPSC trap: bicameralism at the centre began under Act of 1919.

SIMON COMMISSION, 1927

  • Simon Commission was appointed in 1927.
  • It was to review the working of the Government of India Act 1919.
  • The Commission arrived in India in 1928.
  • It had no Indian member.
  • This made it deeply unpopular.
  • Indians protested with the slogan “Simon Go Back.”
  • Lala Lajpat Rai was injured during anti-Simon protests and later died.
  • Simon Commission controversy stimulated Indians to draft their own constitutional proposals.
  • UPSC trap: Simon Commission had no Indian member.

NEHRU REPORT, 1928

  • Nehru Report was prepared in 1928.
  • It was headed by Motilal Nehru.
  • It was not prepared by Jawaharlal Nehru.
  • This is a very common UPSC trap.
  • It was the first major attempt by Indians to draft a constitutional framework for India.
  • It recommended Dominion Status.
  • It recommended parliamentary system.
  • It recommended fundamental rights.
  • It rejected separate electorates in the long run and preferred joint electorates with reservations in some contexts.
  • It recommended responsible government at the centre and in provinces.
  • It recommended a federal structure with residuary powers vested in the centre.
  • Jinnah opposed parts of the Nehru Report.
  • Jinnah later put forward his Fourteen Points in response.
  • UPSC trap: Nehru Report 1928 = Motilal Nehru, not Jawaharlal Nehru.
  • UPSC trap: Nehru Report recommended Dominion Status, not complete independence.

ROUND TABLE CONFERENCES, COMMUNAL AWARD, POONA PACT

  • Three Round Table Conferences were held in London between 1930 and 1932.
  • They discussed constitutional reforms for India.
  • First Round Table Conference was attended by princes, communal groups, and others, but Congress boycotted it.
  • Second Round Table Conference was attended by Gandhi as Congress representative.
  • The issue of minority representation became central.
  • Ramsay MacDonald announced the Communal Award in 1932.
  • The Communal Award gave separate electorates to minorities and also to Depressed Classes.
  • Gandhi opposed separate electorates for Depressed Classes.
  • Dr. B. R. Ambedkar was negotiating for political safeguards for Depressed Classes.
  • The Poona Pact was signed in 1932 between Gandhi and Ambedkar.
  • Poona Pact replaced separate electorates for Depressed Classes with reserved seats in joint electorates.
  • This is a very high-yield constitutional fact.
  • UPSC trap: Poona Pact 1932 modified the Communal Award for Depressed Classes.
  • UPSC trap: Communal Award was announced by Ramsay MacDonald.

GOVERNMENT OF INDIA ACT, 1935

  • Government of India Act 1935 is the most important constitutional act before independence.
  • It is the longest and most detailed British statute on Indian governance.
  • Many features of the Indian Constitution later drew administratively from this Act.
  • It proposed an All-India Federation.
  • The federation was to include British Indian provinces and princely states.
  • However, the federation never came into effect.
  • This is a very high-yield fact.
  • It introduced provincial autonomy.
  • This is one of the most important features.
  • Dyarchy in provinces was abolished.
  • Dyarchy was introduced at the Centre in theory.
  • But the federal/dyarchical centre did not fully come into operation because the federation itself never materialized.
  • Provinces got autonomous governments with ministers responsible to legislatures.
  • The Act provided for bicameralism in some provinces.
  • It extended separate electorates further.
  • It extended franchise but still not universal adult franchise.
  • It provided for establishment of Federal Court in 1937.
  • It provided for creation of Reserve Bank of India.
  • It divided powers between centre and provinces through three lists:
    • Federal List
    • Provincial List
    • Concurrent List
  • This is a very important precursor to present constitutional structure.
  • Residuary powers were vested in the Governor-General.
  • The Act also established provincial autonomy under governors.
  • Governors retained special powers and discretionary powers.
  • This limited true autonomy.
  • UPSC trap: Government of India Act 1935 introduced provincial autonomy.
  • UPSC trap: federal scheme under 1935 Act never fully came into force.
  • UPSC trap: dyarchy in provinces was abolished by 1935 Act.
  • UPSC trap: dyarchy at the centre was proposed by 1935 Act.

PROVINCIAL AUTONOMY, ELECTIONS OF 1937

  • Elections under the Government of India Act 1935 were held in 1937.
  • Congress formed ministries in several provinces.
  • This was the first major experience of Indian leaders running provincial governments under constitutional framework.
  • Provincial autonomy became politically meaningful through these ministries.
  • Congress ministries resigned in 1939 in protest against India being dragged into World War II without consultation.
  • UPSC trap: elections of 1937 were under Government of India Act 1935.

AUGUST OFFER, 1940

  • August Offer was made by the British in 1940.
  • It was offered during World War II.
  • It promised expansion of the Viceroy’s Executive Council.
  • It promised that no future constitution would be adopted without consent of minorities.
  • It was not accepted by Congress.
  • It is important in the chain of constitutional negotiations.
  • UPSC trap: August Offer came in 1940 during wartime.

CRIPPS MISSION, 1942

  • Cripps Mission came to India in 1942.
  • It was sent by the British government during World War II.
  • Sir Stafford Cripps led the mission.
  • It offered Dominion Status after the war.
  • It offered the right of provinces to opt out of the future union.
  • It proposed a constitution-making body after the war.
  • Congress rejected it.
  • Congress objected to the weak and uncertain nature of the offer.
  • The Muslim League also rejected it.
  • Gandhi described it as a “post-dated cheque on a crashing bank.”
  • This is a famous line, though not always necessary in exam answers.
  • Cripps Mission failure was followed by the Quit India Movement.
  • UPSC trap: Cripps Mission offered Dominion Status after the war and possibility of provincial opting out.

WAVELL PLAN AND SHIMLA CONFERENCE, 1945

  • Wavell Plan was proposed in 1945.
  • Lord Wavell was Viceroy.
  • It aimed to reconstitute the Viceroy’s Executive Council.
  • It proposed more Indian representation.
  • Shimla Conference was held in 1945 to discuss the plan.
  • It failed mainly because of disagreement over Muslim representation and League’s claim to represent all Muslims.
  • This was one of the last failed attempts before the Cabinet Mission.
  • UPSC trap: Wavell Plan = 1945 = reconstitution of Viceroy’s Executive Council.

CABINET MISSION PLAN, 1946

  • Cabinet Mission came to India in 1946.
  • It consisted of:
    • Lord Pethick-Lawrence
    • Sir Stafford Cripps
    • A. V. Alexander
  • This is a very high-yield factual trio.
  • The Cabinet Mission rejected the idea of a fully sovereign Pakistan at that stage.
  • It proposed a Union of India dealing with:
    • foreign affairs
    • defence
    • communications
  • Provinces were to retain most other powers.
  • It proposed grouping of provinces into sections.
  • It proposed a Constituent Assembly.
  • This is the most important constitutional outcome.
  • It proposed an interim government.
  • The Constituent Assembly was to frame the Constitution of India.
  • Members were to be indirectly elected by provincial assemblies.
  • Princely states were also to send representatives.
  • Cabinet Mission Plan is the direct constitutional background of the Constituent Assembly.
  • UPSC trap: Cabinet Mission Plan 1946 proposed Constituent Assembly and weak federal union.
  • UPSC trap: Cabinet Mission rejected an immediate sovereign Pakistan formula.

CONSTITUENT ASSEMBLY, FORMATION, ELECTION, NATURE

  • Constituent Assembly was formed in 1946 under the Cabinet Mission Plan.
  • It was not formed under universal adult franchise.
  • This is a very important trap.
  • Members were indirectly elected by provincial legislative assemblies.
  • Seats were allotted roughly on population basis.
  • Different communities had allocated representation in the assembly method.
  • Princely states were to nominate representatives.
  • Total strength originally was 389.
  • Of these, 296 were from British Indian provinces.
  • 93 were from princely states.
  • After partition, the strength reduced to 299.
  • Of these, 229 were from Indian provinces and 70 from princely states in the reconstituted assembly.
  • The Constituent Assembly first met on 9 December 1946.
  • Dr. Sachchidananda Sinha was the temporary chairman.
  • Dr. Rajendra Prasad was elected permanent chairman or President of the Constituent Assembly.
  • H. C. Mukherjee became Vice-President.
  • B. N. Rau was appointed Constitutional Adviser to the Assembly.
  • This is a very high-yield fact.
  • UPSC trap: Constituent Assembly was indirectly elected, not elected by universal adult suffrage.
  • UPSC trap: B. N. Rau was Constitutional Adviser, not Chairman of Drafting Committee.

OBJECTIVES RESOLUTION, 1946

  • Jawaharlal Nehru moved the Objectives Resolution on 13 December 1946.
  • It was adopted on 22 January 1947.
  • Objectives Resolution laid down the philosophy of the Constitution.
  • It declared India to be an independent sovereign republic in the constitutional future.
  • It promised justice, equality, freedom, and safeguards for minorities.
  • It later became the basis of the Preamble.
  • This is a very important constitutional microfact.
  • UPSC trap: Objectives Resolution was moved by Jawaharlal Nehru.
  • UPSC trap: Preamble draws inspiration from Objectives Resolution.

MOUNTBATTEN PLAN, 3 JUNE 1947

  • Lord Mountbatten was the last Viceroy of British India.
  • Mountbatten Plan was announced on 3 June 1947.
  • It accepted partition of British India.
  • It provided for creation of India and Pakistan.
  • It accelerated transfer of power.
  • Provincial assemblies of Punjab and Bengal voted on partition questions.
  • The plan led directly to the Indian Independence Act 1947.
  • UPSC trap: Mountbatten Plan = 3 June 1947 = partition accepted.

INDIAN INDEPENDENCE ACT, 1947

  • Indian Independence Act was passed by British Parliament in July 1947.
  • It came into effect on 15 August 1947.
  • It created two independent dominions:
    • India
    • Pakistan
  • British paramountcy over princely states lapsed.
  • This is a major constitutional fact.
  • Office of Secretary of State for India was abolished.
  • Constituent Assemblies of India and Pakistan became fully sovereign in legislative matters for their respective dominions.
  • The office of Viceroy ended.
  • Governor-General remained in each Dominion.
  • Lord Mountbatten became the first Governor-General of independent India.
  • C. Rajagopalachari later became the only Indian Governor-General of India.
  • This is a high-yield fact.
  • Indian Independence Act is the legal basis of transfer of power.
  • UPSC trap: Indian Independence Act 1947 ended British sovereignty and created two Dominions.
  • UPSC trap: C. Rajagopalachari was the only Indian Governor-General of independent India.

CONSTITUENT ASSEMBLY AFTER INDEPENDENCE

  • After independence, the Constituent Assembly became a sovereign body.
  • It had a dual role:
    • constitution-making body
    • legislative body for the Dominion of India
  • When acting as a legislative body, it was chaired differently.
  • G. V. Mavlankar was associated with the legislative side.
  • The Constituent Assembly continued debates after independence.
  • Partition changed membership composition.
  • Many Muslim League members from areas that became Pakistan left the Assembly.
  • Princely states gradually joined the Indian Union.
  • Their representatives joined the Assembly.
  • UPSC trap: Constituent Assembly functioned both as constitution-maker and dominion legislature.

COMMITTEES OF THE CONSTITUENT ASSEMBLY

  • Committees of the Constituent Assembly are extremely important.
  • Major committees included:
    • Union Powers Committee
    • Union Constitution Committee
    • Provincial Constitution Committee
    • Advisory Committee on Fundamental Rights, Minorities, and Tribal and Excluded Areas
    • Drafting Committee
    • Rules of Procedure Committee
    • States Committee
    • Steering Committee
    • Credentials Committee
    • House Committee
    • Order of Business Committee
  • Jawaharlal Nehru chaired:
    • Union Powers Committee
    • Union Constitution Committee
  • Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel chaired:
    • Provincial Constitution Committee
    • Advisory Committee on Fundamental Rights, Minorities, and Tribal and Excluded Areas
  • J. B. Kripalani chaired one committee relating to fundamental rights in some internal stages? No, keep the standard committee heads clearly.
  • Drafting Committee was the most famous committee.
  • It was set up on 29 August 1947.
  • Dr. B. R. Ambedkar was its Chairman.
  • This is one of the most important constitutional facts.
  • Other members of Drafting Committee originally included:
    • N. Gopalaswami Ayyangar
    • Alladi Krishnaswamy Ayyar
    • K. M. Munshi
    • Syed Mohammad Saadulla
    • B. L. Mitter
    • D. P. Khaitan
  • B. L. Mitter later resigned and was replaced by N. Madhava Rau.
  • D. P. Khaitan died and was replaced by T. T. Krishnamachari.
  • UPSC trap: Dr. B. R. Ambedkar = Chairman of Drafting Committee.
  • UPSC trap: B. N. Rau = Constitutional Adviser, not Drafting Committee Chairman.

DRAFTING OF THE CONSTITUTION

  • B. N. Rau prepared an initial constitutional memorandum and draft material.
  • Drafting Committee used that material and committee reports.
  • The first draft Constitution was published in February 1948.
  • A revised draft was published in October 1948.
  • The Assembly debated the Constitution clause by clause.
  • Debates were detailed and extensive.
  • The Constitution was discussed over nearly three years.
  • Constituent Assembly held 11 sessions.
  • It sat for 165 days.
  • Of these, a large number of days were spent discussing the Draft Constitution.
  • The Assembly considered reports, amendments, minority questions, federalism, judiciary, rights, language, services, and many other issues.
  • UPSC trap: Constitution was not written in a few weeks; it was the result of long committee work and debate.

ADOPTION, ENACTMENT, COMMENCEMENT

  • The Constitution of India was adopted on 26 November 1949.
  • This is Constitution Day in current observance.
  • The Constitution was enacted and given to ourselves on 26 November 1949.
  • However, the full Constitution came into force on 26 January 1950.
  • This is the date of commencement.
  • 26 January was chosen because of the Purna Swaraj Declaration of 1930.
  • This is a very high-yield fact.
  • Some provisions came into force earlier on 26 November 1949 itself.
  • For example, provisions relating to citizenship, elections, provisional parliament, and certain transitional matters came into force then.
  • But the Constitution as a whole commenced on 26 January 1950.
  • India became a Republic on 26 January 1950.
  • Dr. Rajendra Prasad became the first President of India.
  • Dominion of India ended.
  • Republic of India began.
  • UPSC trap: adopted on 26 November 1949, enforced on 26 January 1950.
  • UPSC trap: 26 January was chosen because of Purna Swaraj resolution of 1930.

SALIENT ORIGINAL FEATURES OF THE CONSTITUTION, HISTORICAL LINK TO CONSTITUTIONAL EVOLUTION

  • The Constitution of India was the lengthiest written Constitution in the world at the time of adoption.
  • Originally it had 395 Articles.
  • Originally it had 8 Schedules.
  • It drew from many sources, but it was not a mere copy.
  • It was shaped by Indian conditions and historical experience.
  • Federal structure with strong centre reflected:
    • colonial provincial experience
    • partition context
    • need for unity
  • Parliamentary system reflected British model.
  • Fundamental Rights reflected liberal-democratic traditions and nationalist demands.
  • Directive Principles reflected social justice goals and Irish influence.
  • Independent judiciary reflected rule of law traditions and constitutionalism.
  • Universal adult franchise was a revolutionary decision.
  • This is one of the most important constitutional microfacts.
  • Unlike the colonial reforms, the Constitution adopted universal adult suffrage from the beginning of the Republic.
  • There was no gradual property-based franchise transition after 1950.
  • UPSC trap: Constituent Assembly was indirectly elected, but the Constitution it produced established universal adult franchise.

IMPORTANT PREAMBLE-LINKED HISTORICAL MICROFACTS

  • Objectives Resolution is the philosophical ancestor of the Preamble.
  • Original Preamble described India as:
    • Sovereign
    • Democratic
    • Republic
  • The words Socialist and Secular were added later by the 42nd Amendment in 1976.
  • This is outside 1950, but it is an important trap.
  • Original Constitution in 1950 did not contain the words Socialist and Secular in the Preamble.
  • “We, the People of India” reflects popular sovereignty.
  • This is crucial because the Constitution derives authority from the people, not from British Parliament.
  • UPSC trap: original Preamble did not contain Socialist or Secular.

  • Charter Act 1833 = Company ceases trade, first Governor-General of India.
  • Charter Act 1853 = last Charter Act, open competition principle.

  • Act 1909 = separate electorates.

COMMON UPSC TRAPS AND CORRECTIONS

  • Trap: Portuguese were not the first Europeans in India. Correction: Portuguese were the first Europeans to arrive by sea route in the modern age.
  • Trap: East India Company was established in 1498. Correction: 1498 is Vasco da Gama; Company was founded in 1600.
  • Trap: Regulating Act 1773 created Board of Control. Correction: Board of Control was created by Pitt’s India Act 1784.
  • Trap: Lord William Bentinck was the first Governor-General of Bengal. Correction: Warren Hastings was first Governor-General of Bengal; Bentinck was first Governor-General of India.
  • Trap: Government of India Act 1858 introduced dyarchy. Correction: dyarchy came under Act of 1919.
  • Trap: Indian Councils Act 1861 introduced election. Correction: it introduced nomination of Indians.
  • Trap: Indian Councils Act 1892 clearly used the word election. Correction: indirect election operated in practice, but not formally by that exact statutory language.
  • Trap: Morley-Minto Reforms introduced dyarchy. Correction: they introduced separate electorates.
  • Trap: Montagu-Chelmsford Reforms introduced provincial autonomy. Correction: they introduced dyarchy; provincial autonomy came under Government of India Act 1935.
  • Trap: Government of India Act 1935 established all-India federation in practice. Correction: federation was proposed but never implemented.
  • Trap: Nehru Report was written by Jawaharlal Nehru. Correction: it was headed by Motilal Nehru.
  • Trap: Simon Commission had Indian members. Correction: it had no Indian member.
  • Trap: Constituent Assembly was elected by universal adult franchise. Correction: it was indirectly elected by provincial assemblies.
  • Trap: B. R. Ambedkar was the Constitutional Adviser. Correction: B. N. Rau was Constitutional Adviser; Ambedkar chaired the Drafting Committee.
  • Trap: Constitution was adopted on 26 January 1950. Correction: adopted on 26 November 1949, enforced on 26 January 1950.
  • Trap: India became independent on 26 January 1950. Correction: independence came on 15 August 1947; republic on 26 January 1950.
  • Trap: Original Preamble contained Socialist and Secular. Correction: those words were added later in 1976.
